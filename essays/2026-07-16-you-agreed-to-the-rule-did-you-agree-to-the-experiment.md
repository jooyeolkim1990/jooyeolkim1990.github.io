# You Agreed to the Rule. Did You Agree to the Experiment?

## ICML 2026 and the problem of gatekeeper experimentation

In March 2026, the International Conference on Machine Learning desk-rejected 497 submissions after designated reciprocal reviewers violated its no-LLM review policy.[^1]

Critics called ICML's hidden watermark a trap. Defenders pointed to the agreement: reviewers assigned to Policy A had either chosen the no-LLM rule or said they were comfortable with either policy. Reviewers who strongly preferred the more permissive Policy B were not assigned to A.[^1]

The dispute settled into a familiar question.

Was this entrapment, or enforcement?

ICML's own policy had already supplied another word:

**“Randomization, monitoring, experiment.”**[^2]

Entrapment asks whether ICML induced the violation.

The harder question is why ICML embedded an experiment inside the gate it controlled.

## One policy assignment carried two functions

ICML's 2026 LLM policy created two review conditions.

Policy A prohibited LLM use in reviewing. Policy B allowed limited use for understanding papers, exploring related work, and polishing reviews. Substantive judgment remained with the human reviewer.

Reviewers stated a preference. Authors stated whether their papers required Policy A or allowed Policy B. Each reviewer then received one Actual Policy for all assigned papers.[^2]

Randomization served two stated purposes.

First, program chairs could monitor differences between score distributions under Policy A and Policy B and compensate if systematic differences appeared.

Second, randomization could support an experiment comparing outcomes under the two policies, informing future policy and potentially a peer-reviewed publication.[^2]

Then the policy turned to discipline.

Reviewers had to follow their assigned Actual Policy. Deviation could count as an academic-integrity violation and lead to consequences, including desk rejection of the reviewer's own submissions.[^2]

One assignment therefore carried two institutional functions.

It could enter an experimental comparison.

It was also an enforceable conduct rule.

ICML's public record leaves the randomized arm unidentified and describes the experiment as a comparison of policy outcomes, not violations themselves as the outcome variable.

That narrower factual claim is enough. Experimental comparison and enforceable conduct occupied the same Actual Policy architecture.

## The gate was reciprocal

The architecture did not sit over a detached pool recruited for a policy study.

ICML described reciprocal reviewing as scholarly exchange. Authors were expected to “give back to the community” for the service of being “reviewed by their peers.” Each paper had to nominate a qualified reciprocal reviewer. Failure in reviewer or area-chair duties could put a participant's own papers at risk of desk rejection.[^3]

The matching rules tied the roles together more explicitly. An author who required Policy A for their own paper also had to be willing to follow Policy A as a reviewer.[^2]

That was a reciprocity condition, not automatic assignment to Policy A. A person asking the conference to protect their own paper under Policy A had to be willing to accept the same rule in the review role.

In March, that reciprocal link acquired consequences.

ICML reported 795 violating reviews written by 506 Policy A reviewers. When a designated reciprocal reviewer for an active submission produced one of those reviews, the submission was rejected. The 497 rejected submissions corresponded to 398 reciprocal reviewers.[^1]

The relevant participants were inside a formal exchange. They supplied review labor while their own papers moved through the same conference; review conduct could affect those papers.

Reciprocity itself is ordinary peer-review machinery. A scholarly community can reasonably ask members to contribute labor to a process from which they benefit.

The boundary problem begins when an experimental policy layer enters that exchange without a separately visible route to refuse the experimental part.

**The gate did not sit beside the experiment. The experiment was embedded inside the gate.**

In an earlier essay, I argued that ICML's 2026 response translated selection pressure into behavioral enforcement. Cheap, weak-but-reviewable output strains a review system whose human capacity does not scale at the same speed. Stronger control becomes institutionally attractive.[^4][^13]

This essay asks what happens to the participant relationship when experimentation enters that enforcement-oriented gate.

## Gatekeeper experimentation

I call this **gatekeeper experimentation**: experimentation embedded inside a selection or access process controlled by the same institution that participants are trying to pass through.

The nearest familiar concerns are coercion, constrained consent, and human-participant research ethics. Gatekeeper experimentation starts one step earlier, with institutional position.

The experimenter is also the gatekeeper: one institution controls the opportunity, places people inside the process, compares conditions for experimental learning, and retains authority over compliance and sanctions.

Its diagnostic signal is a missing refusal route.

**Can a participant decline the experimental layer without leaving the underlying gate?**

This is a governance test, not a legal verdict. The public record is insufficient for an IRB, Common Rule, or legal conclusion.

ICML explained policy preferences and Actual Policy assignment. It disclosed the possibility of randomization. It called part of that randomization an experiment.

I found no separately described route in the public materials for a reviewer to decline the experimental comparison while remaining in the reciprocal-review role.

**You agreed to the rule. Did you agree to the experiment?**

Those are not automatically the same institutional act.

## ICML had answers. They answered different questions.

ICML's defenses are serious because they address real problems.

Confidential manuscripts matter. Authors may object to their papers being fed to LLMs. Reviewers should not delegate substantive judgment to a language model. ICML surveyed reviewers, asked for policy preferences, disclosed the possibility of randomization, and used a targeted detection method rather than a generic AI detector. Flagged cases were manually inspected.[^1]

Taken together, these facts show a policy built around real problems and deliberate distinctions.

The answers still belong to different institutional problems.

ICML's own Policy B shows how carefully the conference could distinguish among them. It separated privacy-compliant LLMs from other tools through training and data-retention conditions. Authors could require Policy A or allow Policy B. Some AI-assisted tasks were permitted while judgment, critique, summary, review structure, and review writing remained human responsibilities.[^2]

ICML already knew how to separate kinds of AI use.

Confidentiality governs tools and data handling. Human responsibility governs which tasks can be delegated. Those reasons can support a conduct rule without settling how review activity enters an experimental comparison.

**A reason for the rule is not automatically an authorization for the experiment.**

Enforcement starts after the rule is in place. ICML's hidden-prompt method targeted a specific use pattern, and flagged cases were manually inspected. Reliable evidence can establish that a reviewer breached Policy A.

**Accurate detection can establish the breach. It cannot establish experimental participation.**

Policy design also had a participatory layer.

ICML's policy-design process drew on two anonymous surveys of ICML 2025 reviewers. The first went to 1,100 randomly chosen reviewers and received 150 responses. The second went to a disjoint set of 500 past reviewers and received 74 responses.[^5]

The surveys asked about policy preferences from reviewer and author perspectives, likely noncompliance under Policy A, and likely uses of LLMs in reviewing. ICML used those responses to shape the dual-policy framework and the activities allowed under Policy B.[^5]

The policy was not designed in a vacuum.

The surveys helped decide what policy to build.

The experimental comparison concerned reviewer assignments operating under Actual Policies.

Community consultation does not silently become participant-level agreement to an experiment.

The closest defense is the individual reviewer's agreement to an Actual Policy.

You stated your policy position.

You accepted an assigned rule.

**Did you agree to the experiment?**

## The refusal test

**Visibility is not a refusal route.**

ICML disclosed the experiment. Policy A reviewers had accepted a no-LLM rule. Hidden instructions detected reviewers who nonetheless used LLMs.[^1]

The remaining distinction becomes easier to see in a different gate.

Imagine a high-stakes audition. Contestants agree to the competition rules. The organizer announces that some operating conditions may be varied to compare outcomes and improve future policy. The organizer also retains the power to eliminate contestants who violate their assigned condition.

Putting the experiment in the rulebook makes it visible.

The useful test is whether a contestant can say:

> I will follow the audition rules. I do not want my participation placed in the experimental comparison.

And still remain in the audition.

ICML's public documentation, as I read it, does not describe the reviewer equivalent.

**Meaningful choice is not measured by guessing how many people would say yes. It is measured by whether no is institutionally legible.**

## NeurIPS built the counterfactual in the same year

A practical defense remains: live peer review may make a separate experimental-participation boundary too cumbersome.

NeurIPS 2026 makes that defense harder.

Its official AI-Assisted Reviewing Experiment also operates inside live peer review. Participating reviewers are randomly assigned, per eligible paper, to one of three conditions: no LLM assistance, open-ended LLM assistance, or structured LLM assistance. Area chairs, blind to the assigned condition, assess review quality and helpfulness.[^6]

NeurIPS drew the participation boundary before randomization.

Papers enter only when authors opt in.

Reviewers participate only when they volunteer.

Randomization occurs only among opted-in papers and volunteer reviewers.

The experiment page states that author and reviewer participation is “entirely voluntary” and “will have no bearing on paper decisions or other outcomes of the conference.”[^6]

It also names University of Texas at Austin IRB Protocol #STUDY00009083, states that the protocol was determined exempt under 45 CFR 46.104(2)(i), and provides an IRB contact for questions about participant rights.[^6]

NeurIPS still controlled a gate.

Its 2026 Main Track Handbook links reviewer-author duties to consequences for their own submissions. Reviewer-authors who fail to complete assigned reviews can lose access to reviews of their own papers. The conference also reserves desk rejection as a last-resort sanction for grossly negligent reviewer-authors.[^7]

Reviewer-author duties and sanction authority remained.

**Yet the experiment had an explicit participation boundary.**

The research questions differed. The comparison rests on governance: a flagship conference ran randomized research inside live peer review while keeping participation voluntary and neutral to conference outcomes.

**The relevant governance counterfactual was not hypothetical. NeurIPS built it in the same year.**

## ICML had drawn visible boundaries before

ICML's own recent history makes the missing boundary harder to dismiss as an alien demand.

A January 2026 post on self-ranking said ICML Integrity Chairs had conducted three pilot studies around the conference over the previous three years, beginning with ICML 2023. The post described all three pilots as anonymous, GDPR-compliant, and conducted after the full-paper submission deadline. Their accumulated evidence informed the 2026 self-ranking policy.[^8]

The published ICML 2023 ranking experiment drew its own boundary clearly.

Researchers asked authors with multiple submissions to rank their papers by perceived quality. The study collected 1,342 rankings from distinct authors covering 2,592 submissions. Its acknowledgments thank the ICML Board for providing the opportunity for the experiment.[^9]

The study told participants that survey data would not be used in ICML 2023 decision-making, documented University of Pennsylvania IRB approval, and began analysis only after accept/reject decisions had been announced.[^9]

ICML's grouped account leaves separate IRB or external ethics review for the 2024 and 2025 pilots undocumented. The published 2023 study is enough for a narrower point: ICML's own review environment had already hosted experimentation with a visible boundary from that year's acceptance decisions.

The 2026 LLM policy described experiment and enforcement in one Actual Policy architecture. ICML's public documentation does not explain why that experimental layer needed no comparably visible participation boundary.

**The contrast does not prove misconduct. It makes the missing explanation visible.**

## The peer becomes a compliance subject

ICML first addressed reviewers as peers.

Authors were asked to “give back to the community.” Reciprocal review drew legitimacy from scholarly exchange.

The March enforcement post returned to another relational word: trust. ICML said the community had to protect “our trust in each other.”[^1]

Peerhood was doing institutional work.

It helped create the obligation to review.

Sanction required a different classification.

Violating reviews were removed. Some reciprocal reviewers' own submissions were rejected. In the worst repeat cases, all reviews were deleted and the reviewer was removed from the pool.[^1]

**Peerhood creates the duty. Compliance classifies the failure.**

The same person could move through the system as a peer contributing review labor, a reviewer governed by an experimental policy architecture, a compliance subject, and an author whose own work was exposed to sanction.

Different institutional roles are normal. Here they accumulated faster than the boundaries among them became visible.

Once an institution invokes peerhood and trust to create obligations and defend the process, participant roles cannot remain legible only when the institution needs labor.

The institution designed the sequence.

## The method travelled

NeurIPS returns here for a different reason. Its opt-in AI-Assisted Reviewing Experiment supplied the counterfactual; the hidden-prompt intervention supplies the diffusion path.

Reporting on hidden prompts used to detect prohibited AI use in NeurIPS peer review, *The Transmitter* quoted ICML 2026 Scientific Integrity Chair Nihar Shah saying that he had shared ICML's methodology with the NeurIPS team.[^10]

NeurIPS organizers inserted concealed instructions into papers sent for peer review. Some reviewers objected publicly. One argued that a trap premised on bad faith treated reviewers as suspects and corroded trust. Another said reviewers absorb too much blame because they are the visible point of failure.[^10]

These were separate programs. The AI-Assisted Reviewing Experiment was opt-in research; the hidden-prompt intervention was enforcement.

The available source supports transfer of the hidden-prompt methodology. It does not establish that NeurIPS adopted ICML's A/B policy-randomization experiment.

NeurIPS publicly separated experimental participation from paper decisions in one part of its 2026 review governance.

In another, a contested enforcement technique travelled from ICML into NeurIPS.

**The counterfactual and the diffusion happened in the same institutional ecosystem.**

At least one component of ICML's governance response crossed conference boundaries while the public debate was still struggling to name the boundary problem at ICML itself.

Power makes experiments easier to run.

**It also makes governance techniques easier for others to inherit.**

A flagship institution's conduct can become another institution's starting assumption.

The old defense is, “Everyone does it.”

The more dangerous one is, “ICML did it.”

## The trap debate found the discomfort, but not the object

Public criticism around ICML 2026 identified real injuries.

Entrapment named invisible detection.

Collective punishment named the spillover to co-authors.[^12]

Workload criticism pointed to a system leaning heavily on unpaid scholarly labor.[^11]

False-choice and gatekeeping complaints came closest to the access problem.[^11]

The debate still returned to whether violating reviewers deserved the consequences.

Maybe they did.

A punishable violation does not erase the institutional design surrounding the violator.

ICML had serious reasons for regulating AI use. It distinguished tools, data practices, author preferences, permitted tasks, and human responsibility. It consulted reviewers while designing the policy. It used a targeted detection method and manually inspected flagged cases.

Those answers matter because they show the institution was capable of drawing distinctions.

The public materials I reviewed do not separately explain how a reviewer could decline the experimental comparison while remaining inside the reciprocal gate.

That arrangement has a name.

**Gatekeeper experimentation.**

ICML explained the rule.

It showed the breach.

It documented consultation.

The unanswered act was participation in the experiment.

The counterfactual existed.

The method travelled.

**The precedent travelled before the ethics debate caught up with it.**
